## 1. FEVER Benchmark (Thorne et al., 2018) {#1-fever-benchmark}

### Full Citation
James Thorne, Andreas Vlachos, Christos Christodoulopoulos, and Arpit Mittal. 2018. **"FEVER: a Large-scale Dataset for Fact Extraction and VERification."** In *Proceedings of the 2018 Conference of the North American Chapter of the Association for Computational Linguistics: Human Language Technologies (NAACL-HLT)*, Volume 1 (Long Papers), pages 809-819, New Orleans, Louisiana. Association for Computational Linguistics.

- **ArXiv**: [1803.05355](https://arxiv.org/abs/1803.05355)
- **ACL Anthology**: [N18-1074](https://aclanthology.org/N18-1074/)
- **Citations**: ~1,900+ (Semantic Scholar), making it one of the most cited fact-verification papers

### Key Contribution
FEVER introduced the first large-scale benchmark specifically designed for **evidence-based fact verification**. The dataset contains **185,445 claims** generated by altering sentences extracted from Wikipedia. Each claim is classified as:
- **Supported** (evidence confirms the claim)
- **Refuted** (evidence contradicts the claim)
- **NotEnoughInfo** (insufficient evidence to make a judgment)

Annotators achieved **0.6841 Fleiss kappa** inter-annotator agreement. For Supported/Refuted claims, annotators also recorded the specific evidence sentences.

### Baseline Performance
- Best accuracy with correct evidence: **31.87%**
- Best accuracy ignoring evidence: **50.91%**
- This gap demonstrated the critical importance of evidence retrieval for verification

### How It Has Been Built Upon
FEVER established the dominant paradigm for fact-checking research: **retrieve evidence, then classify**. Virtually every subsequent fact-verification system benchmarks against FEVER. Key evolutionary branches include:
- **FEVER 2.0 (Thorne et al., 2019)**: Shared task with adversarial attacks
- **HoVer (Jiang et al., 2020)**: Multi-hop evidence verification extending FEVER's single-hop design
- **FEVEROUS (Aly et al., 2021)**: Extended FEVER to include structured data (tables)
- **VitaminC (Schuster et al., 2021)**: Contrastive evidence pairs for robustness
- **FactScore (Min et al., 2023)**: Shifted from claim-level to atomic-fact-level verification
- **FacTool (Chern et al., 2023)**: Extended to multi-domain, tool-augmented verification

### Evolution from FEVER to Modern Systems
FEVER's retrieve-then-classify paradigm remains foundational, but modern LLM-based systems have moved beyond its limitations:
1. **From static KB to dynamic retrieval**: FEVER used Wikipedia snapshots; modern systems use live search
2. **From claim-level to atomic-fact-level**: FactScore decomposed claims into atomic facts
3. **From classification to scoring**: Modern systems output fine-grained factuality scores rather than 3-way labels
4. **From single-evidence to multi-source**: FacTool and others verify against multiple tool-retrieved sources

## 2. FActScore (Min et al., 2023) {#2-factscore}

### Full Citation
Sewon Min, Kalpesh Krishna, Xinxi Lyu, Mike Lewis, Wen-tau Yih, Pang Wei Koh, Mohit Iyyer, Luke Zettlemoyer, and Hannaneh Hajishirzi. 2023. **"FActScore: Fine-grained Atomic Evaluation of Factual Precision in Long Form Text Generation."** In *Proceedings of the 2023 Conference on Empirical Methods in Natural Language Processing (EMNLP)*, pages 12076-12100, Singapore.

- **ArXiv**: [2305.14251](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.14251)
- **ACL Anthology**: [2023.emnlp-main.741](https://aclanthology.org/2023.emnlp-main.741/)
- **Code**: [github.com/shmsw25/FActScore](https://github.com/shmsw25/FActScore)

### Key Contribution
FActScore introduced the **Decompose-Then-Verify** paradigm that became the dominant approach for LLM factuality evaluation. The key insight: rather than evaluating an entire generation holistically, **break it into atomic facts** and verify each independently.

The FActScore metric = percentage of atomic facts in a generation that are supported by a reliable knowledge source.

### Methodology
1. **Decomposition**: Break a long-form generation into individual atomic facts
2. **Retrieval**: For each atomic fact, retrieve relevant passages from a knowledge source
3. **Verification**: Use a strong LM to determine whether each atomic fact is supported
4. **Scoring**: FActScore = (# supported atomic facts) / (# total atomic facts)

### Key Results
- Automated FActScore estimation achieves **<2% error rate** compared to human evaluation
- Demonstrated significant variation in factuality across LLMs (e.g., ChatGPT vs. PerplexityAI)
- Established person biography generation as a canonical evaluation domain

### How It Has Been Built Upon
FActScore's decompose-verify approach became the backbone of most subsequent factuality evaluation systems:
- **VeriScore (Song et al., 2024)**: Extended FActScore with claim extraction and per-claim verification scoring
- **SAFE (Wei et al., 2024, Google DeepMind)**: Search-Augmented Factuality Evaluator building on FActScore's decomposition
- **OpenFActScore (2025)**: Open-source reimplementation with improved accessibility
- **FaStFact (2025)**: Faster, stronger long-form factuality evaluation building on FActScore
- **Long-form factuality benchmarks**: Most 2024-2025 factuality papers use FActScore or variants as baselines

### Evolution: From FActScore to Current State
The decompose-verify paradigm has evolved in several directions:
1. **Claim granularity debates**: The "Decomposition Dilemmas" paper questioned whether finer decomposition always helps
2. **Multi-source verification**: Moving beyond Wikipedia to web search, knowledge graphs, code execution
3. **Efficiency improvements**: FaStFact and others address FActScore's computational cost
4. **Domain generalization**: From biographies to arbitrary domains (code, math, scientific claims)

## 3. FacTool (Chern et al., 2023) {#3-factool}

### Full Citation
I-Chun Chern, Steffi Chern, Shiqi Chen, Weizhe Yuan, Kehua Feng, Chunting Zhou, Junxian He, Graham Neubig, and Pengfei Liu. 2023. **"FacTool: Factuality Detection in Generative AI -- A Tool Augmented Framework for Multi-Task and Multi-Domain Scenarios."**

- **ArXiv**: [2307.13528](https://arxiv.org/abs/2307.13528)
- **Code**: [github.com/GAIR-NLP/factool](https://github.com/GAIR-NLP/factool)

### Key Contribution
FacTool introduced a **task-agnostic and domain-agnostic** framework for detecting factual errors in LLM-generated text using **external tools**. Unlike FEVER (which uses static Wikipedia) or FActScore (which uses retrieval from a knowledge source), FacTool leverages diverse tools:
- **Google Search** for factual QA
- **Google Scholar** for scientific literature verification
- **Python interpreter** for code verification
- **Wolfram Alpha / calculators** for mathematical claims

### Methodology
1. **Claim extraction**: Extract verifiable claims from LLM output
2. **Tool query generation**: Generate appropriate tool queries for each claim
3. **Tool execution**: Execute queries against relevant tools (search, code, math)
4. **Evidence collection**: Gather tool responses as evidence
5. **Verification**: Use LLM to judge claim-evidence agreement

### Evaluation Domains
FacTool was evaluated across four fundamentally different tasks:
- Knowledge-based QA
- Code generation
- Mathematical reasoning
- Scientific literature review

### How It Has Been Built Upon
FacTool established the **tool-augmented verification** paradigm:
- **OpenFactCheck (2024)**: Building/benchmarking customized fact-checking systems
- **Loki (2024)**: Open-source tool for fact verification building on similar principles
- **A Unified Framework for Factuality Evaluation of LLMs (EMNLP 2024)**: Unified multiple approaches including tool-augmented methods
- **CRITIC (Gou et al., 2023)**: Parallel work on tool-interactive self-correction (see below)

### Key Innovation: Domain Independence
FacTool's major advance over FActScore was demonstrating that the same framework could verify claims across fundamentally different domains by selecting appropriate tools. This moved fact-checking from a "NLP-only" task to a general "AI verification" task.

## 4. CRITIC (Gou et al., 2023) {#4-critic}

### Full Citation
Zhibin Gou, Zhihong Shao, Yeyun Gong, Yelong Shen, Yujiu Yang, Nan Duan, and Weizhu Chen. 2023. **"CRITIC: Large Language Models Can Self-Correct with Tool-Interactive Critiquing."**

- **ArXiv**: [2305.11738](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.11738)
- **OpenReview**: [Sx038qxjek](https://openreview.net/forum?id=Sx038qxjek)

### Key Contribution
CRITIC demonstrated that LLMs can **self-correct their own outputs** through iterative interaction with external tools. The key insight: rather than using tools only for verification (as in FacTool), CRITIC uses tool feedback in a **correction loop** -- generate, critique with tools, revise, repeat.

### Methodology
1. **Initial generation**: LLM produces an initial response
2. **Tool-interactive critiquing**: LLM generates critiques of its own output, using tools (search engines, calculators, code interpreters) to verify specific aspects
3. **Revision**: Based on tool feedback, LLM revises its output
4. **Iteration**: Process repeats until the output passes verification

### Benchmark Results
CRITIC was evaluated on three tasks:
- **Free-form question answering**: Improved factual accuracy through search-based verification
- **Mathematical program synthesis**: Used code execution for self-correction
- **Toxicity reduction**: Demonstrated broader applicability beyond factuality

Results showed consistent performance improvements across all tasks, with external tool feedback being crucial (self-correction without tools was much less effective).

### How It Has Been Built Upon
CRITIC helped establish the **self-correction with external feedback** paradigm:
- **Self-Refine (Madaan et al., 2023)**: Parallel work on iterative self-refinement
- **LLLM Cannot Self-Correct Reasoning Yet (Huang et al., ICLR 2024)**: Important counterpoint showing limitations of self-correction without external feedback, validating CRITIC's tool-use approach
- **CriticBench (2024)**: Benchmark specifically for critique-correct reasoning
- **Reflexion (Shinn et al., 2023)**: Related iterative self-improvement approach

### Key Insight: External Feedback Is Essential
A crucial finding validated by subsequent work (Huang et al., 2024): LLMs cannot reliably self-correct through intrinsic reasoning alone. External tool feedback (as in CRITIC and FacTool) is necessary for reliable fact-verification. This finding has major implications for autonomous AI systems.

## 5. Evidence-Based Temporal Fact Verification (Barik et al., 2024) {#5-temporal-fact-verification}

### Full Citation
Anab Maulana Barik, Wynne Hsu, and Mong Li Lee. 2024. **"Evidence-Based Temporal Fact Verification."**

- **ArXiv**: [2407.15291](https://arxiv.org/abs/2407.15291)
- **Submitted**: July 21, 2024; revised August 19, 2024

### Key Contribution
This paper addresses an **understudied dimension** of fact verification: claims containing **temporal information**. While FEVER and FactScore focus on static factual claims, real-world claims often involve time-sensitive assertions ("X was the president in 2020", "Company Y was founded before Company Z").

### Methodology
1. **Temporal-aware embeddings**: Retrieves top-k relevant evidence sentences using embeddings that capture temporal relationships
2. **Event modeling**: Models events in both claims and evidence sentences
3. **Temporal reasoning via LLM**: Leverages large language models to determine temporal support/refutation
4. **Curated temporal datasets**: Two new datasets designed for learning time-sensitive representations that capture semantic relationships among events AND their chronological proximity

### Key Results
- Significantly enhances accuracy of temporal claim verification over prior methods
- Advances state-of-the-art on temporal reasoning in fact-checking

### Position in the Evolution
This work represents a **specialization branch** of the FEVER lineage -- rather than improving general-purpose fact-checking, it tackles a specific dimension (temporality) that general systems handle poorly. It highlights a fundamental limitation: most decompose-verify systems treat facts as static, ignoring the temporal dimension entirely.

### Implications for Modern Systems
- Temporal claims are common in news, finance, and legal domains
- Standard retrieval-based approaches fail when temporal reasoning is required
- Integration of temporal-aware embeddings with LLM reasoning suggests a path toward more nuanced verification

## 6. Solving Freshness in RAG (Grofsky, 2025) {#6-freshness-in-rag}

### Full Citation
Matthew Grofsky, D.Eng CISSP. 2025. **"Solving Freshness in RAG: A Simple Recency Prior and the Limits of Heuristic Trend Detection."**

- **ArXiv**: [2509.19376](https://arxiv.org/html/2509.19376)
- **Date**: September 2025

### Key Contribution
Addresses **temporal failures in RAG systems** -- the problem that retrieval-augmented generation systems often return outdated information because they lack temporal awareness. This is directly relevant to fact-verification: a verification system that retrieves stale evidence will produce incorrect verdicts.

### Methodology: Four-Stage Pipeline
1. **Data Normalization**: Maps disparate logs to standardized schema with UTC timestamps
2. **Embedding**: Uses Sentence-BERT (all-MiniLM-L6-v2) producing 384-dimensional vectors
3. **Weekly Topic Tracking**: K-means clustering per ISO week with trend labels (emergence, growth, decay, drift, stable)
4. **Time-Aware Retrieval**: Fused scoring formula: `score(q,d,t) = alpha * cos(q,d) + (1-alpha) * 0.5^(age_days/h)`

### Key Results
| Metric | Score | Baseline |
|--------|-------|----------|
| As-of correctness | 1.00 | - |
| Latest@10 accuracy | 1.00 | 0.00 |
| Trend F1 (heuristic) | 0.08 | - |

- Recency prior achieves **perfect accuracy** on freshness tasks
- Heuristic trend detection performs poorly (0.08 F1), proving that trend detection needs more than simple clustering
- Default parameters: alpha=0.7 (semantic weight), half-life h=14 days

### Critical Finding
The paper demonstrates that **simple recency weighting is extremely effective** for freshness, but **detecting topic evolution trends is hard**. This has direct implications for fact-verification systems that need to determine which version of a fact is "current."

### Note on Lineage
This paper does NOT reference FEVER, FactScore, FacTool, or CRITIC. It positions itself within **Temporal Information Retrieval** literature (Metzler et al. 2009, Blei & Lafferty 2006) rather than the fact-checking literature. This represents a **parallel evolution** -- the RAG/retrieval community addressing freshness independently of the fact-checking community.

### Implications for Fact Verification
- Verification systems need temporal awareness in their retrieval component
- A lightweight recency prior (half-life decay) can dramatically improve evidence freshness
- The approach is model-agnostic and requires no base model retraining

## 7. Decomposition Dilemmas (Hu, Long, & Wang, 2024) {#7-decomposition-dilemmas}

### Full Citation
Qisheng Hu, Quanyu Long, and Wenya Wang. 2024. **"Decomposition Dilemmas: Does Claim Decomposition Boost or Burden Fact-Checking Performance?"**

- **ArXiv**: [2411.02400](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.02400)
- **Venue**: Accepted at **NAACL 2025** (Main Conference)
- **Code**: [github.com/qishenghu/Decomp_Dilemmas](https://github.com/qishenghu/Decomp_Dilemmas)

### Key Contribution
This paper provides the **first comprehensive analysis** of whether the Decompose-Then-Verify paradigm (popularized by FActScore) actually helps or hurts fact-checking performance. The answer: **it depends**, and the paper systematically identifies when and why.

### Four Categories of Decomposition Errors
1. **Omission of Context Information**: Missing core claims, key details, or logical relationships (causal, comparative, contrastive)
2. **Ambiguity**: Vague language including unclear pronouns, unspecified entity references, incomplete names
3. **Over-Decomposition**: Excessive fragmentation creating redundant or repetitive sub-claims
4. **Alteration of Original Meaning**: Introduction of fabricated or contradictory information

### Key Quantitative Results

**Claim-Level Verification (WICE dataset, Minicheck verifier):**
| Method | Balanced Accuracy |
|--------|-------------------|
| Baseline (no decomposition) | **80.01%** |
| VeriScore decomposition | 74.74% |
| WICE decomposition | 76.81% |
| FactScore decomposition | 71.11% |

**Response-Level Verification (FELM dataset, Minicheck verifier):**
| Method | Balanced Accuracy |
|--------|-------------------|
| Baseline (no decomposition) | 56.84% |
| VeriScore decomposition | **58.97%** |
| WICE decomposition | 58.81% |
| FactScore decomposition | 56.29% |

### Critical Findings
1. **Decomposition helps weaker verifiers but hurts stronger ones**: ChatGPT's accuracy improves with decomposition, while GPT-4's performance declines
2. **Complexity matters**: For complex inputs (WICE_long), decomposition improves performance; for simple inputs (FELM_short), decomposition degrades results
3. **Sub-claim count threshold**: As sub-claim count increases, decomposition's impact shifts from positive to negative
4. **Trade-off**: Accuracy gains from simplification are offset by noise from decomposition errors

### Datasets Used
| Dataset | Samples | Avg Tokens |
|---------|---------|-----------|
| WICE | 382 | 33.65 |
| CLAIMDECOMP | 400 | 35.82 |
| FELM | 184 | 66.74 |
| BINGCHAT | 396 | 351.9 |

### Impact on the Field
This paper is a crucial **corrective** to the field's assumption that decomposition is always beneficial. It directly challenges the FActScore paradigm and has influenced subsequent work:
- **ACL 2025**: "Optimizing Decomposition for Optimal Claim Verification" (Lu, 2025) builds on these findings
- **DecMetrics (2025)**: Structured claim decomposition scoring incorporating lessons from decomposition noise
- Informs the design of VeriScore's more conservative claim extraction approach

## 8. Multi-Agent Debate for Factuality (Du et al., 2023/2024) {#8-multi-agent-debate}

### Full Citation
Yilun Du, Shuang Li, Antonio Torralba, Joshua B. Tenenbaum, and Igor Mordatch. 2023/2024. **"Improving Factuality and Reasoning in Language Models through Multiagent Debate."**

- **ArXiv**: [2305.14325](https://arxiv.org/abs/2305.14325) (May 2023)
- **Venue**: **ICML 2024** (41st International Conference on Machine Learning)
- **Code**: [github.com/composable-models/llm_multiagent_debate](https://github.com/composable-models/llm_multiagent_debate)
- **Project**: [composable-models.github.io/llm_debate/](https://composable-models.github.io/llm_debate/)

### Key Contribution
Introduced the **"society of minds" approach** where multiple LLM instances propose, debate, and iteratively refine their responses to reach consensus. This represents a fundamentally different paradigm from decompose-verify: instead of decomposing claims, **multiple agents debate** the correctness of the whole response.

### Methodology
1. Multiple LLM instances independently generate initial responses
2. Each agent receives all other agents' responses and reasoning
3. Agents critique each other and revise their own responses
4. Process repeats for multiple rounds until consensus emerges
5. Works on black-box models with identical prompts across all tasks

### Benchmark Results (using GPT-3.5-turbo)
| Task | Single Agent | Multiagent Debate | Improvement |
|------|-------------|-------------------|-------------|
| Arithmetic | 67.0% | 81.8% | +14.8 pp |
| GSM8K | 77.0% | 85.0% | +8.0 pp |
| Chess Move Optimality | 74% | 100% | +26.0 pp |
| Biography Factuality | 66% | 74% | +8.0 pp |
| MMLU | 64% | 71% | +7.0 pp |

GSM8K with summarization: 85.0% -> 87.0% (+2.0 pp)

### How It Has Been Built Upon (2024-2026)

**Direct Extensions:**
- **Sparse Communication MAD (2024, EMNLP Findings)**: Improves efficiency by pruning redundant critiques via embedding similarity; cuts token costs by up to **94.5%** with <2% performance loss
- **S2-MAD (2025)**: "Breaking the Token Barrier" -- further efficiency improvements
- **Tool-MAD (2026)**: Multi-agent debate with **heterogeneous external tools** (search API, RAG modules); achieves up to **5.5% accuracy improvement** over standard MAD
- **A-HMAD (2025)**: Adaptive Heterogeneous Multi-Agent Debate with diverse specialized agents and dynamic debate mechanisms
- **M3MAD-Bench (2026)**: Unified benchmark for systematic evaluation of MAD methods across domains and modalities; found that simply using heterogeneous models does not yield substantial performance leaps

**Applied to Fact Verification:**
- **M-MAD (ACL 2025)**: Multidimensional multi-agent debate for machine translation evaluation
- **Multi-Agent Debate for Fine-Grained Reasoning in Math (ACL Findings 2025)**

### Key Insight: Consensus vs. Decomposition
The debate paradigm offers a complementary approach to decompose-verify:
- **Decompose-verify** (FActScore lineage): Break claims into atoms, verify each independently
- **Debate** (Du et al. lineage): Keep claims whole, let multiple agents argue about correctness
- The Decomposition Dilemmas paper suggests debate may avoid decomposition's noise problems
- Tool-MAD (2026) combines both: agents debate while each having access to different verification tools

## 9. VeriScore (Song, Kim, & Iyyer, 2024) {#9-veriscore}

### Full Citation
Yixiao Song, Yekyung Kim, and Mohit Iyyer. 2024. **"VERISCORE: Evaluating the factuality of verifiable claims in long-form text generation."** In *Findings of the Association for Computational Linguistics: EMNLP 2024*, pages 9447-9474, Miami, Florida, USA.

- **ArXiv**: [2406.19276](https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.19276)
- **ACL Anthology**: [2024.findings-emnlp.552](https://aclanthology.org/2024.findings-emnlp.552/)
- **Code**: [github.com/Yixiao-Song/VeriScore](https://github.com/Yixiao-Song/VeriScore)

### Key Contribution
VeriScore addresses a fundamental limitation of FActScore and SAFE: those systems assume **every claim is verifiable**. In reality, LLM-generated text contains many unverifiable claims (opinions, subjective assessments, vague statements). VeriScore focuses exclusively on **extracting and evaluating verifiable claims**, making it applicable to diverse long-form tasks beyond biography generation.

### Methodology (Three-Step Pipeline)
1. **Claim Extraction**: Extract only *verifiable* claims (not all atomic facts) from generated text. This is the key methodological difference from FActScore
2. **Evidence Retrieval**: Uses Serper API to retrieve evidence from Google Search for each extracted claim
3. **Claim Verification**: Binary classification -- each claim is judged as supported or unsupported based on retrieved evidence

### VeriScore Formula
VeriScore = (# supported verifiable claims) / (# total verifiable claims extracted)

### Key Results
- Human evaluation confirms VeriScore's extracted claims are **more sensible** than FActScore and SAFE across **8 different long-form tasks**
- Evaluated **16 different LLMs** across multiple tasks
- **GPT-4o** is the best-performing model overall
- **Open-weight models** (especially Mixtral-8x22) are closing the gap
- **Critical finding**: An LM's VeriScore on one task does NOT necessarily correlate to its score on a different task -- factuality varies by task and domain

### Relationship to FActScore
VeriScore is a direct evolution of FActScore, addressing its key limitation:

| Aspect | FActScore | VeriScore |
|--------|-----------|-----------|
| Claims extracted | All atomic facts | Only verifiable claims |
| Knowledge source | Wikipedia | Google Search |
| Primary domain | Biography generation | Multiple long-form tasks |
| Assumes all claims verifiable | Yes | No |
| Can evaluate | Factual precision only | Factual precision on verifiable content |

### How It Has Been Built Upon
- **MedScore (2025)**: Domain-adapted claim decomposition and verification for medical free-form answers, directly building on VeriScore's methodology
- **FaStFact (2025)**: Faster, stronger long-form factuality evaluation that improves on VeriScore's efficiency
- **Decomposition Dilemmas (NAACL 2025)**: Uses VeriScore as one of its primary baselines for claim decomposition evaluation
- **DecMetrics (2025)**: Structured claim decomposition scoring building on VeriScore's claim extraction approach

### Key Evolutionary Patterns

#### Pattern 1: From Static to Dynamic Evidence
- **2018 (FEVER)**: Wikipedia snapshots as evidence
- **2023 (FacTool, CRITIC)**: Live search, code execution, calculators
- **2024 (SAFE, VeriScore)**: Google Search for real-time evidence
- **2025 (Freshness in RAG)**: Temporal-aware retrieval with recency priors
- **2026 (Tool-MAD)**: Heterogeneous tools with adaptive retrieval

#### Pattern 2: From Holistic to Atomic to Selective
- **2018 (FEVER)**: Whole-claim verification (Supported/Refuted/NEI)
- **2023 (FActScore)**: Atomic fact decomposition -- verify every atom
- **2024 (VeriScore)**: Selective -- only verify *verifiable* claims
- **2025 (Decomposition Dilemmas)**: Discovered decomposition isn't always beneficial
- **2025+ (DecMetrics, Optimized Decomposition)**: Adaptive decomposition based on input complexity

#### Pattern 3: From Single Agent to Multi-Agent
- **2018-2023**: Single model/pipeline approaches
- **2023 (Du et al.)**: Multi-agent debate for consensus
- **2024**: Sparse communication, efficiency improvements
- **2025-2026 (Tool-MAD, A-HMAD)**: Heterogeneous agents with different tools/specializations

#### Pattern 4: From General to Domain-Specific
- **2018 (FEVER)**: General Wikipedia facts
- **2023-2024**: General-purpose LLM factuality
- **2025+**: Domain-specific verification (MedScore for medicine, ClimateCheck for climate, temporal for time-sensitive claims)

#### Pattern 5: External Feedback > Self-Correction
- **CRITIC (2023)**: Showed tool feedback dramatically improves self-correction
- **Huang et al. (ICLR 2024)**: "LLMs Cannot Self-Correct Reasoning Yet" -- validated that external feedback is essential
- **Tool-MAD (2026)**: Combines debate (social feedback) with tools (external feedback)

### Open Problems (as of 2026)
1. **Decomposition quality**: When/how to decompose remains unsolved (Decomposition Dilemmas)
2. **Temporal reasoning**: Most systems still can't handle time-sensitive claims well
3. **Cross-task generalization**: VeriScore showed factuality doesn't transfer across tasks
4. **Efficiency vs. accuracy**: Multi-agent debate is effective but expensive; sparse communication helps
5. **Multimodal claims**: FEVER 9 is just beginning to address image-text claims
6. **Freshness**: RAG systems need temporal awareness, but trend detection remains hard

## Bonus: SAFE (Wei et al., 2024) -- Key Bridge Paper {#bonus-safe}

### Full Citation
Jerry Wei, Chengrun Yang, Xinying Song, Yifeng Lu, Nathan Hu, Jie Huang, Dustin Tran, Daiyi Peng, Ruibo Liu, Da Huang, Cosmo Du, and Quoc V. Le. 2024. **"Long-form factuality in large language models."** *NeurIPS 2024.*

- **ArXiv**: [2403.18802](https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.18802)
- **Code**: [github.com/google-deepmind/long-form-factuality](https://github.com/google-deepmind/long-form-factuality)
- **Venue**: **NeurIPS 2024**

### Key Contribution
SAFE (Search-Augmented Factuality Evaluator) bridges the gap between FActScore's academic rigor and practical deployment needs. It adds a **multi-step reasoning process** with Google Search verification and extends evaluation to an **F1 score framework** balancing precision and recall.

### Methodology
1. **Decomposition**: LLM breaks response into individual factual claims (following FActScore)
2. **Multi-step reasoning**: For each fact, generate search queries, retrieve results, reason about support
3. **Classification**: Facts labeled as supported or unsupported
4. **F1 scoring**: Extends beyond precision (FActScore) to include recall -- how many expected facts were covered

### Key Results
- **72% agreement** with crowdsourced human annotators on ~16,000 individual facts
- **76% win rate** on 100 random disagreement cases (SAFE more often correct than humans)
- **20x cheaper** than human annotators
- **LongFact benchmark**: GPT-4 generates thousands of questions spanning 38 topics
- **13 LLMs** evaluated across 4 model families (Gemini, GPT, Claude, PaLM-2)
- Finding: Larger models generally achieve better long-form factuality

### Why SAFE Matters
SAFE demonstrated that **automated fact-checking can be more accurate than crowdsourced human evaluation** while being dramatically cheaper. This shifted the field's mindset from "human evaluation is gold standard" to "automated evaluation can exceed human quality at scale."
